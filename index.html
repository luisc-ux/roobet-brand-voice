01 — The Foundation

THE 5 PRINCIPLES

These aren't personality traits. They're writing decisions. Each one tells you what to do, and what not to do.

01
SHORT, not padded

We have the reader for seconds. If it can be one sentence, make it one. Lead with the point. No filler before the hook.

Before
Don't forget, you have funds waiting in your account! Log in now and see what's available.
After
Your balance is just sitting there. UCL kicks off in an hour.
02
LOUD, not yelling

Energy comes from what you say, not from !! or ALL CAPS. One exclamation point per piece, maximum. Confidence doesn't need to shout.

Before
DON'T MISS OUT!! Huge rewards are waiting RIGHT NOW!!!
After
$30K. Three games. The leaderboard closes Sunday.
03
SHARP, not smug

The reader is always in on the joke. We're the fun friend, not the try-hard. We never make not-playing feel like a failure.

Before
Only smart players are taking advantage of this. Are you one of them?
After
Zeus built a roulette table. It's unhinged.
04
REAL, not performative

No forced slang. No chasing trends. When we say something, we mean it. Our copy travels internationally. It can't rely on single-market references.

Before
No cap bestie, this promo is absolutely fire fr fr 🔥
After
Crash just hit 1000x. Someone was in it.
05
BOLD, not reckless

We play with real money and real emotions. We hype the win. We never mock the miss. Responsible language is non-negotiable, and it's still possible to sound like Roobet.

Before
You can't afford to miss this. Losers don't take chances. Winners do.
After
One winner goes to Monaco. The leaderboard is open.

On edge & innuendo

Roobet has permission to live close to a line. Copy can carry a double reading, a knowing edge, a second meaning, as long as it's smart enough that the joke works either way.

The test: does it hold up without the wink? If you remove the second reading and it's still a good line, you're in the right place. If it only works as innuendo, it's too much.

Where it works / where it doesn't

CRM body copy, onsite inbox: there's context, the player is already in
Sports and game campaigns: competitive context holds the edge naturally
Welcome emails and ACQ: too early. The player doesn't know us yet.
Subject lines: risky without context. Only if the rest of the email makes it land.
Anything that could be read as punching at the player: never.

02 — Calibration

TWO TONE MODES

Roobet's voice doesn't change, but the volume does. Different moments call for different settings.

CONTROLLED PLAYFUL

Promo campaigns · New games email · Network promotions · Sportsbook

  • "Zeus built a roulette table. It's unhinged."
  • "Crash just hit 1000x. Someone was in it."
  • "Something wicked has a $50K prize pool."
  • "$30K. Three games. No situationship energy. Pick your match."
QUIET CONFIDENCE

Onboarding · Retention · VIP · Reward confirmation

  • "It's already in your account. Use it."
  • "Your balance is ready. The match isn't."
  • "One winner goes to Monaco. The leaderboard is open."
  • "Your account's been quiet. We put something in it."

CRM: Player Journey Stages

Not all existing players are the same. Where someone is in their journey shapes the tone, not just whether they have an account.

New
First days on platform.
Warmer, more guided. Still Roobet but less insider. Help them find their footing.
Active
Regular players.
Full voice. Assumes familiarity. Lead with the prize or the moment. Skip the intro.
VIP
High-value players.
Quiet Confidence. Premium feel. Less promotional, more personal. Never mass-market in tone.
Lapsed
Players who've gone quiet.
Quiet Confidence. Acknowledge the gap without making it awkward. Lead with what's waiting for them.
Churned
Players who've left.
Handle with care. Don't make assumptions about why they left. One specific reason to come back beats any amount of "we miss you" energy.

03 — The Constraints

COPY BY CHANNEL

Constraints are not limitations. They're the brief. Work with them.

EMAIL SUBJECT LINE
~50 characters on mobile
  • Hook in the first 4-6 words. The reader decides in under a second.
  • No exclamation point here. Save it for the preheader.
  • One emoji maximum. Make it earn its place. Filters detect presence, not type, so the limit is on quantity.
  • Never open with the campaign name. Lead with the tension, the moment, or the prize.
  • Username personalisation token can be used — but it's not a warm-up. It needs to sit alongside a real hook, not replace one.
  • No dollar amounts or prize figures in the subject line or preheader. This is the biggest signal pushing emails to the Promotions tab. Move amounts to the body.
  • Never say "chase" in CTAs.

~85 characters
  • Add excitement, don't repeat the subject line. The reader just read it.
  • Give the second beat: the detail that earns the hook.
  • Urgency, intrigue, or a punchline. Make it pop.
  • This is where the ! lives, if anywhere.

26 characters maximum

  • Short and punchy. No repetition with the body copy below.
  • Structure: lead with action or prize, not "earn" or "experience".

  • One thought. One action.

05 — Who Does It Well

BRANDS THAT
GET IT

These aren't brands we copy. They're brands that calibrate the taste. When in doubt about tone, ask yourself: does this feel more like them, or like everyone else?

WENDY'S
Self-aware · Dry · Never tries too hard
They roast competitors, respond to fans in character, and never break tone, not even for a second. The wit is always earned, never forced. And they never, ever sound corporate.
  • "We don't freeze our beef. Unlike some people we know."
  • "Sorry, we only serve fresh beef. Try a different fast food chain for the frozen stuff."
What we borrow: The self-awareness. The willingness to have a point of view. The dry delivery that makes the joke land without winking at the camera.
RYANAIR
Anti-corporate · Brutally honest · Owns the joke
They lean into every complaint, every meme, every bad take about them, and make it funnier. Their voice is consistent because it's built on honesty, not spin. They say what everyone's already thinking.
  • "You get what you pay for. Cheap flights, not a massage."
  • [Responding to a complaint about legroom] "You're right, we'll pass on the message to your legs."
What we borrow: The anti-corporate confidence. The refusal to over-explain or over-apologize. The trust that the audience gets the joke.
DUOLINGO
Consistent voice · Purposeful chaos · Never breaks character
The owl is unhinged, but always coherently unhinged. Every platform, every format, every campaign stays in character. The chaos is strategic. The rules are strict. It just doesn't look like it.
  • "We noticed you haven't practiced in a while. The owl noticed too."
  • [Leaning into the meme] "Miss a lesson and see what happens."
What we borrow: The consistency. The character-first thinking. The understanding that being memorable is a long game, and you build it one consistent interaction at a time.

None of them sound like a casino. That's the point.

The iGaming space defaults to hype, caps, and hollow urgency. These three brands, in completely different industries, built recognition by sounding like themselves, consistently, even when it was harder. Roobet's job is the same: be so unmistakably Roobet that nothing else sounds right.

06 — Protecting the Voice

HANDLING
FEEDBACK

Feedback comes from everywhere. Here's how to read it.

✓ Always take it

The Brief

Prize amounts, game mechanics, features, eligibility, dates, segmentation: anything about what we're communicating.

No filter needed. They know what needs to be said. Your job is to say it in Roobet's voice. When in doubt about any detail, confirm with the stakeholder before writing.
→ Read it first

The Direction

"Make it more exciting." "This feels flat." "Can we add more energy?" Style notes, phrasing requests, alternative copy from the requestor.

Don't apply it literally. Figure out what they actually want: more urgency, a bigger focus on the prize, a warmer opening. Then solve it the Roobet way. If someone says "more engaging," that means more specific and more unexpected. Not louder.
Patterns to watch for

These patterns can appear in copy at any stage of the process. When you spot them in a draft — your own or anyone else's — fix them before submitting.

Red flags in any draft

  • Multiple exclamation points or ALL CAPS energy
  • "Don't miss out!" / "Huge rewards await!" / "This is your chance!"
  • Longer, vaguer, and less specific than the brief
  • Hollow adjectives: incredible, amazing, seamless, elevate, curated
  • Could have been written by any casino, not specifically Roobet
  • Structure flipped: "If Y, get X" instead of "Get X if Y"
  • Em dashes used anywhere in the copy

The rule

The brief always wins.
The direction gets interpreted, not copy-pasted.

07 — Before You Submit

QUICK
REFERENCE

A checklist for every piece of copy before it goes out. Click to check off.

Before you start

Check all info is clear. If anything is missing or ambiguous, clarify with your CRM contact before writing.

  • Total number of copies required confirmed?
  • Campaign name and all game names confirmed and accurate?
  • Promotion dates confirmed?
  • Are the mechanics clearly explained in the brief?
  • Does the theme make sense and align with the game sheet?
  • For onsites: confirmed with stakeholder whether to go desire-only or include mechanic summary?
  • Is there anything specific that must appear in the copy? Confirmed with stakeholder?

Before you submit

Read the copy back as if you're seeing it for the first time. Check tone and brief accuracy together.

  • Could a competitor have written this? If yes, it's not Roobet yet.
  • Does it lead with the reward, not the condition? "Get X if Y", not "If Y, get X."
  • Does the copy feel live, not like an announcement?
  • Is the tone right for the player stage? (New / Active / VIP / Lapsed / Churned)
  • For longer campaigns: no repeated openers, structures, or urgency devices from previous rounds?
  • Does the copy answer What, When, and How?
  • Are exclusivity periods included where required?
  • Do prize amounts match across brief, landing page, email, and onsite?
  • Does the LP have full detail? (The email can play a little. The LP cannot.)
  • Character limits respected for every channel and language?
  • No dollar amounts or prize figures in subject line or preheader?
  • Anything still to confirm with the stakeholder before this goes out?

WHAT THE AUDIENCE RESPONDS TO

This is the psychological layer behind the copy. Every piece should pull at least one of these levers.

✓ They respond well to

Exclusivity: something not everyone gets
Progression: a sense of building toward something
Live experiences: this is happening now, not later
Competition: leaderboards, rankings, being ahead
Momentum: "you're on a streak", "keep going"
Scarcity: limited spots, closing soon, one left
Fast feedback: immediate reward, instant result

✗ They switch off from

Corporate language: anything that sounds like a press release
Generic casino terminology: "spin to win", "place your bets"
Overexplaining mechanics: if it needs three sentences, simplify
Long intros: if the hook isn't in line one, there is no hook

ADDITIONAL RULES FROM PRACTICE

Rules that have emerged from live campaign work. Apply alongside the five principles.

Negative framing leads
Elimination, game cuts, round endings, and "X players left" framings never open copy. The primary narrative is always winning, prize pools, and player opportunity. Secondary messaging handles the rest.
Announcement energy
Copy should make the player feel they're stepping into something live, not receiving a notification. Lead with what's already happening. Create momentum, not announcements.
Cross-asset accuracy
Prize amounts, dates, and key figures must match across all assets: brief, landing page, email, and onsite. Check everything against everything before submitting, not just within individual pieces.
Consistency across rounds
For longer campaigns, never recycle openers, subject line structures, or urgency devices from previous rounds. Each piece should feel fresh. If round two sounds like round one, it needs a rewrite.
LP vs. email scope
The landing page must carry all the detail. The email can play a little and doesn't need to cover everything. If there's anything specific that must appear in the email, confirm with the stakeholder before writing.

WORDS TO AVOID

Don't miss out!
Generic casino panic. Roobet doesn't beg. Be specific about what they're missing.
Huge rewards await
Vague and performative. What rewards? How huge? Replace with the actual number.
This is your chance!
Every casino says this. It means nothing. The copy itself should create urgency.
Chase [the win]
Not permitted in CTAs. Full stop.
Stake
Avoid entirely, both as a verb and as a reference to the competing brand.
Log in (to logged-in users)
They're already in. Telling them to log in is confusing at best, lazy at worst.
Contendientes
Avoid in Spanish copy. Doesn't land naturally in LATAM markets.
Amazing / incredible
Overused modifiers. Replace with a specific fact. Facts are more convincing than adjectives.
We miss you!
Corporate-warm, not Roobet-warm. Try: "Your account's been quiet. We put something in it."
Exclusive offer
Means nothing if overused. If it's truly exclusive, prove it: say who it's for and why.
Click here
Tell them what they're clicking toward. "Claim your $20" beats "click here" every time.
WIN BIG / FREE!!! / GUARANTEED
Spam triggers. Immediately signals generic casino marketing and kills deliverability.
LIMITED TIME OFFER
If there's urgency, express it specifically. "Closes Sunday" beats "limited time" every time.

08 — Inbox Placement

EMAIL
DELIVERABILITY

Rules that protect inbox placement without compromising the voice. These apply to every email before it goes out.

Subject Line

No $ amounts or currency figures. No % figures. One emoji max. Filters detect the presence of an emoji, not which one, so the restriction is on quantity not type. Lead with narrative or curiosity, not financial value. Personalization tokens are fine and help open rates.

Pre-header

Read alongside the SL by Gmail's algorithm. Don't use the PH to sneak in the numbers you cut from the SL. That workaround doesn't work. Story or intrigue only, not financial value.

Body Copy

Dollar amounts are fine in the body for existing subscribers. The body is less algorithmically scanned for tab sorting. Don't stack prize figures, opt-in language, and "Promotions Page" in the opening lines. Lead with narrative. Mechanics come after.

Structure

Always include a plain text alternative. Maintain a healthy image-to-text ratio. An email that's one large image with minimal text reads as spam. Real text content alongside visuals is non-negotiable.

WORDS TO AVOID IN SL & PRE-HEADER

These are known filter triggers. They don't need to appear together. Any of them in the SL or PH is a signal that pushes toward the Promotions tab.

free / bonus / prize
Primary filter triggers. Use "reward", "drop", or describe the specific thing instead.
win / jackpot / guaranteed
High-risk regardless of context. Rephrase around the game, the moment, or the player.
$[any amount] in SL or PH
Financial figures in the subject line are the single biggest promo-tab signal. Move amounts to the body.
cash / exclusive offer / act now
Generic commercial language. Be specific about what, when, and who.
limited time / click here
Both are spam flags. Use specific dates and specific CTAs instead.

✓ Safe in SL & Pre-header

Tournament, round, stage, points, game, play
Tonight, this week, specific dates (e.g. "closes Sunday")
Username personalization token
Sport and team references
Narrative hooks and tension, not financial value

Pre-send Check Tools

Every email gets a deliverability review before it sends. Copy-level review happens at drafting. Technical testing (SPF, DKIM, DMARC validation) must be confirmed before any major send.

GlockApps: inbox placement testing (Gmail Primary vs. Promotions vs. Spam). Primary tool for pre-send testing.
mail-tester.com: free spam score with specific flagged reasons. Good for a quick check.
Google Postmaster Tools: domain reputation monitoring. Set up once, monitor ongoing around every send.

Rules in this section are based on Gmail's tab-sorting algorithm behaviour and standard ESP deliverability research. For the latest guidance, check Google's Promotions tab documentation and Litmus deliverability resources.

08 — Staying Human

AI DETECTION
CHECK

AI is a useful starting point. But a Roobet email written by AI and left unedited will read like one. Here's how to check your copy before it goes out.

SIGNALS YOUR COPY IS TOO AI

Run this check on any copy before it goes to review. If more than two of these are true, rewrite before submitting.

Em dashes everywhere
The most reliable AI signal. "Something, anything, like this" is a pattern AI defaults to constantly. Replace with a period or restructure the sentence. No em dashes in Roobet copy, ever.
Multiple exclamation points
AI interprets "make it exciting" as adding !! or !!!. One per piece, maximum. If the copy needs punctuation to feel energetic, the copy isn't working.
Longer than the original
AI expands. Roobet compresses. If the AI version is wordier than what you briefed, it hasn't found the point yet.
Hollow adjectives
"Incredible," "amazing," "seamless," "elevate," "curated," "unparalleled." None of these do any work. Replace with a specific fact or cut entirely.
Structure flipped
AI defaults to "If Y, get X." Roobet leads with the reward: "Get X if Y." Check every bullet in How to Play and every CTA construction.
Could be any casino
The core test. Read it back and ask: could this live on a Stake email? A Bet365 landing page? If yes, it hasn't been made into Roobet yet.

Use AI for

First draft structure and rough angles
Generating multiple options to react to
Localization first pass before human review
Checking mechanics and bullet point logic
Reformatting or adapting approved English copy

Never let AI do

Write the final subject line without a human edit
Make tone decisions ("make this more exciting")
Write VIP or high-value player communications
Replace the brand voice judgment call
Submit copy that hasn't been read by a human first

External Detection Tools

If you want to run a quick check before submitting, these tools flag AI patterns. They're a useful second opinion, not a final verdict. Human judgment always wins.

Originality.ai
Checks for AI patterns and flags specific sentences. Useful for longer copy blocks.
Copyleaks
Good for localized copy. Supports multiple languages including Spanish and Portuguese.
Your own ear
Read it out loud. If it sounds like a press release or a generic casino, it needs another pass.

AI writes a draft. You write Roobet.

Localizations

LOCALIZATIONS

Same voice, different language. Open each section when you need it.

GENERAL RULES
Applies to all languages
▲
Localize, don't translate

Sound like Roobet in that language. Not a word-for-word render of English.

Character limits are non-negotiable

Languages run longer. Tighten the message, not the rules. Check before considering the task complete.

Exclamation points vary by language

EN: one max. ES: even more restrained. They read unnatural faster. Never fake energy with punctuation.

CTAs: conjugated form, 2-3 words max

"Juega ahora" not "Jugar ahora". Conjugated imperative, not infinitive. Max 3 words.

Promo names stay in English

In italics. Highlighted yellow in source so CRM can link. Mr. Roo is always Mr. Roo.

No regional lock-in

Unless targeting one market specifically, copy must work across all markets sharing that language.

Caps follow the language

ES and BR: no title-case in subject lines. Don't force the English convention.

esports: always lowercase e

Never eSports, e-sports, or Esports. Every language.

🇲🇽🇦🇷
SPANISH
Mexico · Argentina · LATAM
▼
Neutral tuteo. Nothing regional

Must feel natural simultaneously in Buenos Aires, Mexico City, and Bogotá. No voseo: agarras not agarrá, decides not decidís.

Exclamation points

Even more restrained than English. Each one needs to earn it. Full inverted punctuation sets: ¡Así se juega!

Formality

Day-to-day: informal tú, plural ustedes. For T&Cs: confirm with requester if usted is needed.

Numbers and dates

Keep number formatting consistent with English ($30,000 not $30.000). Different LATAM markets use different conventions — consistency with graphics wins. Dates: DD/MM.

Prize pool

"$X en premios" for Roobet-owned campaigns. "Pozo total" may appear in network/Pragmatic promotions. Keep consistent within each campaign.

False friends

"Billion" in English = "mil millones" in Spanish. "Billón" in Spanish = trillion. Always double-check large numbers.

Key terminology

Free Bets: Apuesta Gratis / Apuestas Gratis. Opt-in: Apúntate. How to Enter: Cómo unirte. Autobet: Autoapuesta. Leaderboard: ranking or tabla de clasificación.

Keep in English

Sportsbook, lobby, game names, client seed, stop on loss, stop on profit, jackpot tier names (Mini/Minor/Major/Mega), free chips.

PORTUGUESE
Brazil
▼
Runs longer than English

Character limits are even more critical. Never translate word-for-word. Prioritize the message over literal meaning.

Formality and register

Use você for day-to-day. Not European Portuguese tu. "A gente" over "nós" for conversational naturalness.

False cognates

"Atualmente" ≠ "actually". If you mean "atualmente", the English source should say "currently" or "nowadays".

Numbers and dates

Thousands: dot ($1.000). Decimals: comma (1,7). Dates: DD/MM. Ranges: "a" between dates (28/08 a 10/09).

🇯🇵
JAPANESE
Japan
▼

No nominalizing verbs. No repetition. Write succinctly. Add a space after question marks.

Newsletters: spoken language

Conversational written form, not formal document language.

Word order and meaning

Japanese and English have different word orders. Don't mirror the English structure. Translate context, not words.

Numbers and dates

Date: AA/MM/DD. Alphanumeric characters for numbers. Currency: $.
